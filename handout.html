1 Introduction

Duplicates in data are a major concern for organizations seeking to make accurate and efficient use of information. When identical or nearly identical records appear multiple times in a dataset, they can distort analytics, increase storage costs, and reduce trust in data-driven processes (OWOXBI (2024); Airbyte (2025)). Research has shown that unmanaged duplicates can lead to incorrect conclusions, particularly in scenarios like scientific studies or business intelligence where precision is essential (Biotechnology Information (2023); PubMed (2025)). Effectively identifying and addressing data duplication requires technical strategies and careful consideration of data sources, structures, and intended uses (ScienceDirect (2020); Coursera (2025)). This session provides an overview of why duplicates occur, the challenges in detecting them, and solutions for ensuring data quality in practical settings.

2 Understanding Duplicate Data

Duplicate data occurs when the same information is represented multiple times within a dataset or across multiple databases, often with slight variations (DataLadder (2021)). Ideally, each record should uniquely represent one entity, such as a customer or product. However, several factors contribute to duplicates: Lack of Unique Identifiers: Without unique keys like Social Security Numbers for individuals or Manufacturer Part Numbers for products, it becomes difficult to distinguish individual records (DataLadder (2021)).

Absence of Validation and Integrity Checks: Even with unique identifiers, errors in data format or missing integrity constraints (e.g., incorrect patterns or lengths) allow duplicates to enter the system (Oracle (2024)).

Human Error: Manual data entry errors are common, with error rates estimated around 400 per 10,000 entries, leading to unintentional duplicates (DataLadder (2021)).

Data Heterogeneity: Records collected or merged from different sources often have inconsistent formats, naming conventions, or incomplete data, complicating duplicate detection (Oracle (2024)).

Systemic Causes: Organizations often have multiple departments or external partners independently entering or syncing data, creating redundant copies (Oracle (2024)).

These causes highlight why duplicate data is prevalent and why managing uniqueness is critical to maintaining data accuracy, reducing storage needs, and ensuring reliable analytics.

3 Challenges in Detecting and Handling Duplicates

Detecting and handling duplicates in data presents several notable challenges. First, duplicates may not be exact copies but rather near-duplicates, differing slightly due to typographical errors, formatting variations, or incomplete entries (Naumann (2007)). This makes simple equality checks insufficient for effective detection. Instead, similarity measures and thresholds must be carefully chosen to balance precision—correctly identifying duplicates—and recall—finding as many duplicates as possible (Naumann (2007)). Second, scalability poses a major issue. Large datasets containing millions of records make pairwise comparisons computationally expensive; hence, efficient algorithms such as the Sorted Neighborhood method, which partitions and sorts data to reduce comparison scope, are widely used (Naumann (2007)). Maintaining efficiency while ensuring high-quality duplicate detection is critical in practice. Third, heterogeneity complicates detection: data from multiple sources often differ in structure and content standards, necessitating flexible approaches that handle missing values, inconsistent attribute naming, and varied data types (Oracle (2024)). Finally, organizational challenges such as insufficient data governance policies and lack of standardized data entry practices can exacerbate duplication problems, limiting the effectiveness of technical solutions (Oracle (2024)). These challenges underline the complexity of deduplication efforts and the need for comprehensive strategies combining algorithmic methods with organizational best practices.

4 Methods and Solutions for Handling Duplicates

Addressing duplicate data effectively involves a combination of detection algorithms, automated tools, and organizational practices. Two primary approaches dominate the field: deleting duplicates or consolidating them into a single, authoritative record (Datablist (2018)). Deletion is straightforward and involves algorithms identifying duplicate entries and removing redundant copies, leaving only one instance. This method works well when duplicates are exact or near-exact and no conflicting data exists (Datablist (2018)). Consolidation or merging becomes necessary when duplicates contain complementary or conflicting information. Advanced deduplication tools analyze records, select a “master” record that has the most complete data, and merge attributes from duplicates to enrich that record. Conflicting data fields can be resolved either algorithmically or via manual review (Datablist (2018)). Technically, various algorithms help detect duplicates, including exact matching on keys, fuzzy matching for typographical differences, phonetic algorithms for name variations, and similarity scoring for complex cases. Tools like TreeSize, AllDup, and Datablist provide user-friendly interfaces for scanning large data repositories and removing or merging duplicates efficiently (TreeSize (2021); Datablist (2018)). Best practices include implementing data governance to standardize entry and validation, setting up automated deduplication workflows, and periodically scanning data stores to maintain quality. Combining algorithmic and human-in-the-loop processes ensures high accuracy in deduplication.

5 Best Practices for Managing Duplicate Data

Effective management of duplicate data requires a strategic approach combining technology, processes, and people. Key best practices involve: Standardizing Data Entry: Enforce standardized formats and validation rules during data entry to prevent duplicates from entering the system initially. Consistent use of unique identifiers and data categories reduces errors and inconsistencies (Meetergo (2025)).

Implementing Automated Deduplication Tools: Leverage software tools that integrate with data management systems to regularly scan for duplicates and either merge or remove them based on predetermined rules. Solutions like SAP Master Data Governance use AI-driven methods to detect and resolve duplicates efficiently (Meetergo (2025)).

Centralizing Data Management: Consolidate data into centralized repositories to avoid fragmentation and discrepancies across multiple systems. This reduces redundancies caused by isolated data silos (SpareTech (2023)).

Employee Training and Awareness: Regularly train staff on data quality importance and proper data handling procedures. Educated employees are less likely to introduce duplicates through manual errors (Meetergo (2025)).

Continuous Monitoring and Improvement: Establish ongoing data quality monitoring with audits and reports. Use feedback loops to refine deduplication criteria and processes, adapting to evolving data sources and business needs (Oracle (2024)).

By combining these best practices, organizations can maintain high-quality data, optimize storage cost, and improve decision-making reliability.

6 Conclusion

In summary, managing duplicates in data is essential for maintaining data integrity, efficiency, and reliability across various applications. Identifying duplicates is challenging due to variations in data quality, scale, and complexity, but modern techniques like fuzzy matching and partitioning strategies offer effective solutions (Naumann (2007)). Organizations must adopt robust methods for detection, removal, and consolidation, supported by automated tools and strong data governance frameworks (Datablist (2018); Oracle (2024)). Prevention through standardized data entry, centralized management, and continuous monitoring remains the best long-term strategy to avoid duplication and its associated costs (Meetergo (2025); SpareTech (2023)). Ultimately, an integrated approach that combines technology, process optimization, and staff training ensures that data duplicates do not undermine business intelligence, research accuracy, or operational excellence.

7.2 Code

Code snippets are based on the example dataset “diamonds.” For other datasets, the code must be adapted accordingly.

#Check if duplicates are present.
any(duplicated(diamonds))
#Check how many duplicates there are.
sum(duplicated(diamonds))
#Check which ones the duplicates are.
which(duplicated(diamonds))
#Get an exact list of the duplicates.
diamonds[duplicated(diamonds), ]
#Delete all duplicates. Only unique data remains.
diamonds_no_dupes <- diamonds |>
  (\(x) x[!duplicated(x) & !duplicated(x, fromLast = TRUE), ])
#Delete all duplicates. One copy of each dataset remains.
diamonds_unique <- diamonds %>% distinct()
#Mark all duplicates.
diamondsMarked <- diamonds |>
  transform(is_dup = duplicated(diamonds))
#Delete all duplicates, but keep the most recent entry.
diamonds_unique <- diamonds |>
  (\(x) x[!duplicated(x), ])
